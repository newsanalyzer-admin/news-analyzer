# Bias & Fallacy Reference Card
### NewsAnalyzer — Article Curation Project

---

## How to Use This Card

You'll be reading short news excerpts (1–3 paragraphs). Your job is to find passages where the **reasoning is flawed or unfair** — not necessarily false, but where the author is pushing you toward a conclusion using a trick instead of honest argument.

For each suspicious passage, underline it on the article sheet and fill out the annotation worksheet. Use the names on this card to identify what type of reasoning error it is.

**You do not need to find something in every article.** Some articles are clean — mark those as "No bias found."

---

## PART 1 — Logical Fallacies

These are errors in the *structure* of an argument — the reasoning is broken regardless of whether the facts are true. You'll recognize most of these from rhetoric and composition.

---

### Ad Hominem
**The trick:** Attacking the *person* making the argument instead of addressing the argument itself.
> *"We can't trust Senator Walsh's budget proposal — he's been divorced twice."*

The senator's personal life has no bearing on whether the budget math is sound.

---

### Straw Man
**The trick:** Misrepresenting someone's position to make it easier to attack. The author argues against a version of the opponent's view that the opponent never actually held.
> *"Critics of the new highway say we should just let roads crumble and strand commuters."*

The critics said nothing of the sort — the author invented an extreme position.

---

### False Dilemma  *(also: False Dichotomy)*
**The trick:** Presenting only two options when more exist. Forces a choice between extremes by hiding the middle ground.
> *"Either we pass this bill as written, or we abandon any hope of addressing climate change."*

There are obviously other bills, other approaches.

---

### Slippery Slope
**The trick:** Claiming that one event will inevitably lead to a chain of extreme consequences, without demonstrating why those steps must follow.
> *"If we allow background checks on gun purchases, the next step is a federal gun registry, and after that the government will confiscate every firearm in the country."*

Each step in the chain needs its own argument. None is provided.

---

### Appeal to Authority
**The trick:** Using an authority figure's opinion as proof — especially when the authority is outside their area of expertise, anonymous, or when experts disagree.
> *"Nine out of ten doctors recommend this supplement."* (Which doctors? Paid by whom?)

Note: Citing a legitimate expert in their actual field is *not* a fallacy. The fallacy is misusing authority as a shortcut to avoid the real argument.

---

### Red Herring
**The trick:** Introducing irrelevant information to distract from the actual issue.
> *"The congressman is calling for an investigation into the contract — but let's not forget that his own district has one of the highest crime rates in the state."*

The district's crime rate has nothing to do with the contract investigation.

---

### Circular Reasoning  *(also: Begging the Question)*
**The trick:** The conclusion is assumed in the premise. The argument proves itself using itself.
> *"The Bible is true because it is the word of God, and we know it is the word of God because the Bible says so."*

---

### Affirming the Consequent  *(Formal Fallacy)*
**The trick:** A formal logic error. The structure is: *"If A then B. B is true. Therefore A."* But B could be true for many other reasons.
> *"Criminals avoid honest work. This man is unemployed. Therefore he's probably a criminal."*

Being unemployed does not imply criminality — the logic form is broken.

---

### Denying the Antecedent  *(Formal Fallacy)*
**The trick:** Another formal logic error. The structure is: *"If A then B. A is false. Therefore B is false."* But B could still be true for other reasons.
> *"If the company were guilty, they'd hire expensive lawyers. They didn't hire expensive lawyers. So they must not be guilty."*

Innocence isn't the only reason to skip expensive lawyers.

## PART 2 — Cognitive Biases

These are not argument structure errors but *thinking patterns* that distort how information is presented or framed. You'll recognize these from CBT — many are the same distortions that therapy teaches patients to watch for in their own thinking.

---

### Confirmation Bias
**The trick:** Selectively citing evidence that supports one conclusion while ignoring or dismissing contradicting evidence. The author presents a one-sided picture as if it were the full story.
> *An article on immigration that cites every crime committed by an undocumented person over the past year, while mentioning no statistics about crimes committed by citizens.*

CBT connection: This is the same pattern as "mental filtering" — focusing only on negatives (or positives) and filtering out the rest.

---

### Framing Effect
**The trick:** Using word choice and framing to steer the reader's emotional response, independent of the facts. The same facts described differently lead to different conclusions.
> *"The government is spending $2 billion on veterans' healthcare"* vs. *"The government is wasting $2 billion on a bloated veterans' bureaucracy."*

Both could describe the same program. The framing is doing the persuasive work, not the facts.

Watch for: loaded adjectives ("reckless," "radical," "brave," "common-sense"), passive vs. active constructions, what's mentioned first.

---

### Anchoring Bias
**The trick:** Introducing a specific number or reference point early to influence how readers evaluate subsequent information. The "anchor" sticks even when it's arbitrary.
> *"Originally priced at $500, this program now costs taxpayers just $200."*

The $500 anchor makes $200 feel like a bargain — but $200 may still be far too much.

In journalism: an article might open with an extreme estimate of a cost or casualty figure, then "revise down" to a number that still seems reasonable only because of the anchor.

---

### Availability Heuristic
**The trick:** Overweighting vivid, recent, or emotionally memorable examples as if they represent the statistical norm. If a story is dramatic, it gets treated as typical.
> *"After three high-profile attacks at airports this year, a new poll shows 70% of Americans now believe airports are unsafe."*

Three incidents ≠ a pattern. But the vividness of the stories makes the risk feel larger than the data supports.

CBT connection: "Catastrophizing" and "magnification" draw on the same mechanism — the brain assigns higher probability to frightening scenarios because they're vivid.

---

### Bandwagon Effect  *(also: Appeal to Popularity)*
**The trick:** Arguing that something is correct, good, or worth doing simply because many people believe it or do it. Popularity is treated as proof.
> *"A majority of Americans now support this policy — it's time Congress listened."*

Majorities have been wrong. Popularity is not evidence of correctness.

Distinct from Appeal to Authority: Bandwagon uses *quantity* (everyone believes it); Appeal to Authority uses *status* (an expert believes it).

## Quick Checklist

When you underline a passage, ask:

1. **Is an argument being attacked instead of the person making it?** → Ad Hominem
2. **Is someone's position being exaggerated or distorted?** → Straw Man
3. **Are only two options presented when more exist?** → False Dilemma
4. **Is a chain of extreme consequences predicted without evidence?** → Slippery Slope
5. **Is an authority cited outside their expertise, or suspiciously?** → Appeal to Authority
6. **Is something irrelevant being introduced to distract?** → Red Herring
7. **Does the argument prove itself using itself?** → Circular Reasoning
8. **Is the logic form broken (If A then B, therefore...)?** → Formal Fallacy
9. **Is only one side of the evidence being shown?** → Confirmation Bias
10. **Is word choice doing more work than the facts?** → Framing Effect
11. **Was a big number introduced early to make a smaller one seem reasonable?** → Anchoring Bias
12. **Is one dramatic example being treated as a trend?** → Availability Heuristic
13. **Is popularity being used as proof?** → Bandwagon Effect

## A Note on Difficulty

**Easy:** The fallacy is obvious — a glaring insult, a blatant either/or.
**Medium:** Requires careful reading. The trick is there but not shouted.
**Hard:** You'd have to know the definition well to catch it. When in doubt, mark it "Hard" — that's valuable information too.

**If two reasonable people would disagree** about whether a passage contains a bias, that is worth noting. Write "debatable" in the explanation column.
